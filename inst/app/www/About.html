Preface

This document contains information describing the context, motivation and technical implementation of the Pittsburgh Transit Propensity Tool. As we aspire to have this document be accessible both to the lay and technical public alike, each section is tabbed into two sections: “Big Picture” and “Technical.” The former is written for a lay person interested in understanding this project from a “Big Picture” perspective and consequently efforts are made to remove as much technical jargon, citations or mathematical notation to ensure reader understanding. In contrast, the Technical tab is written for a research audience looking to understand, comment on and possibly build off of this work.

Introduction

Big Picture

Cars are expensive. Whether considering the financial cost of buying, insuring, fueling and maintaining a car, the cost of human life and injury caused annually by vehicle accidents, their contribution to environmental problems like global warming and air pollution, or more aesthetic concerns regarding noise and city views, the current state of car-dependency that is common in much of the U.S. presents a serious problem for the economic, physical and mental well-being of its current and future residents.

Charting a path from the current state of infrastructure in U.S. cities to a place of independence from cars is no simple feat, however. The car has been the dominant mode of transit in the U.S. for decades and zoning and land use patterns have reinforced the place of the car as a necessity within the average American’s daily life. Decoupling the car from everyday life will then require some measure of understanding where and why individuals must use cars so that policy makers can more precisely target funding for transit, pedestrian and bike infrastructure and incentives.

The car has been the dominant mode of transit in the U.S. for decades (cite census data) and zoning and land use patterns have reinforced the place of the car as a necessity within the average American’s daily life (Guillen and Komac 2020) . Take a different direction from here?

With this principle in mind, we propose to develop and adapt the the Propensity to Cycle Tool (PCT) (Lovelace et al. 2017) which offers an intuitive way to understand where and to what extent residents of the UK utilize cycling as a mode of transit for their daily work commute. Our project aims to adapt and extend the work of the PCT to the city of Pittsburgh in order to map how and where individuals move around the city.
